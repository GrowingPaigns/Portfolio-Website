A Different Perspective On Illusions

In the fall semester of 2021, I took an AR/VR development course at my university. Being familiar with video game releases, I found there was a significant lack of unique, experimental titles available for VR systems. Thats not to say there were none available, I could list a handful of titles which made use of vr capabilities in unique and interesting ways. But online stores were heavily saturated with action-shooters that ranged from realistic-tactical experiences, to cheaply thrown together multiplayer arcade shoot-em-ups, and there certainly wasn't any unique puzzle experiences, like the games I list next.

A few of the most prominent non-VR titles I remember playing were ones which featured optical illusion gameplay mechanics. Antichamber, Superliminal, and Monument Valley are three games which I continually rave about that both astounded me, and inspired me to become a creative developer myself. I wanted to try to bring that same sort of experience to the highly immersive world of VR.

Future Plans. . .
Since this project was always more of a learning experiment than anything, I eventually want to try and use headset cameras in order to capture the movement of players fingers. I want to use this to allow players to "pinch-to-pick-up" in-game objects, just like the illusion where you're squishing someones head, or "holding" the Leaning Tower of Pisa between your fingers.

(Check out the video below to see the illusion in action!)

The summarized form of the mechanic is this:


There are two raycasts sent out from the center of the player's headset.

One identifies objects that can be interacted with. This raycast is responsible for "picking up" the aimed at object while also gathering all transform elements/variables of the object (scale, position, orientation) the moment the player presses the "grab" button.

The other raycast (named "targetForTakenObj") ignores all interactable objects and passes through them to detect the boundary of the surrounding environment in-front of the player.

The "picked up" object then has its position changed to the position of "targetForTakenObj" (i.e. it is made a child object of the player's "crosshair" at the distance it was from the player when they picked it up). This all occurs the first moment (frame) after the player presses the grab button. From the second frame onward, as the player looks around the room, the position of the object will continually update as we try to place the object flush against the surface our second ray is aiming at (against the boundary of the room).

In order to create the illusion where the object scales up the further it gets from the player while seeming to remain the same size in the player's "hands". . . We first have to gather the current distance between the player's headset, and the object we picked up at its current, updated position.

objectDist = Vector3.Distance(mainCamera.position, takenObject.position);
We can divide that distance by the initial distance between the camera and the object, which we recorded "right before it was picked up", in order to calculate how much we need to scale up our object by.

(objectDist/originalDistance) = updatedScale
By then multiplying the original "local-scale" transform of the object by the updatedScale calculation, we can achieve the effect of making an object physically larger or smaller while appearing to remain the same size in the player's "hands".

Of course, thats not all there is to it, the actual calculations for updating the scale and size of objects deal with things like Quaternions and Euler Angles, but this is the basic breakdown of the mechanic.

Gradual, Incremental Improvements:


When starting this project, I had some surface level experience with Unity 2D/3D and generally how physics worked while using in-engine "components". What I didn't know is that Unity's built-in physics components are dog water at handling interactions when it comes to slightly more complex terrain like ramps (or when trying to link multiple player controls together - such as wall jumps, and sliding).

Thankfully, our lord and savior, Sebastian Lague, created an incredibly comprehensive, and professionally programmed tutorial series on just this issue, and how to set up an entire custom 2D Platformer Physics System in Unity. While this tutorial is nearing 8 years old, it is still one of the only released tutorials that covers this topic, and thereby, deserves this mention (at the very least). With his tutorial series, I have successfully set up the custom physics system, and began the process of transferring over some of the mechanics seen in my video above, which he did not cover how to create (because there's a million and one other tutorials/resources that do). I am currently aiming for an alpha release by mid-2024 to start getting player feedback on the project. A Problem of Our (My) Own Creation:

The problems we faced while building this project were more-so tedious than challenging to figure out; and, despite what the title of this section says, we most certainly did NOT innovate upon anything.
Because of both, our inexperience with the language Arduino systems use, and the inclusion of a material we hadn't worked with prior to this point (as well as the usual time crunch of the school semester), the code that I mostly produced was pure garbage.

The main issue we faced was with how the pre-made LED "sheet" of lights we bought for this project had been soldered together. This "sheet", as it turned out, was just one long snaking strip of LEDs connected by plastic. This material, and all other important ones, are listed in the related github repository (linked at the end of this section). Now we could've done something like cut the strips apart, re-solder them, and build a new backing to the lights so they were not laid out in a snaking pattern. But after needing to solder 1 other thing throughout the entire semester, none of us were confident that we wouldn't ruin the (at the time) $50 strip of lights (it is now even more expensive).

Instead, we (I) opted for the much more inefficient route of creating the file linked on my GitHub repository with 1350 lines of code that, looking back, could have easily been reduced to under 500 lines (the stress of wanting to get a project done really takes a toll on your ability to produce something marginally better than a complete dumpster fire).

The "Solution":

Due to the snaking pattern of the lights, I had to calculate a different distance for each row of lights the shot moved up in order to turn on the next light, and turn off the previous one.

The way I handled this was by gathering the “top” position of the player's ship, and subtracting that position from the integer value of the start of that line of LED’s. Essentially, I was getting the distance between the start of that line of LEDs, and the current position of the top light of the ship.

(shipTopPos - lightTempAtStartOfRow) = distToStartOfRow
Then, I would make that distance value negative, multiply it by 2, and subtract that value again from the original position of the top of the ship. This calculation determines the distance between the top of the ship, and where the "shot" should first appear (right above the top of the ship).

(distToStartOfRow * -2) + shipTopPos = firstShotDist
After that, this calculation basically just repeats until the "shot" goes off the sheet of LEDs, but for each line after the first, the shot uses its own previous position, instead of the top of the ship, in order to determine where it needs to move next.

(distToStartOfRow * -2) + shotPrevPos = firstShotDist

An Amazing Experience

Over summer (June-Aug, 2023) I acted as a UI/UX Development and World Building intern at Castix LLC. In this role, I began by implementing a client-chat feature, and some quality of life chat additions for the company's upcoming MMO title.

This task required I utilize Unity's TextMeshPro components, and related pseudo-HTML stylings, in order to detect when a URL gets posted to the established chat system. After detecting the URL, I had to set up a security feature which would either mark a link as "Whitelisted", "Blacklisted", or "Unlisted".

A whitelisted URL would appear as a user normally expects. Underlined/blue in color, and it would navigate the user to the related website after being clicked on. In relation to this specific type of link, I was also tasked with creating a pop-up graphic that asked the user to confirm if they actually wanted to navigate away from the game, to the page. This graphic would further give the user the option to add the URL to their "personal-whitelist" before navigating, so that the confirmation window will no longer pop up in the future, making the user automatically navigate to the related page.



An unlisted URL simply gets underlined, but remains white in color, and cannot be interacted with.



A blacklisted URL never gets sent to the chat, and the user gets a server message telling them that blacklisted URLs cannot be posted.



Beyond this, while completing the above task, I also implemented a "character counter" in the chat that restricted a player's message to 160 characters in length. This led to the need to also add a few other quality of life features like, shortening the names of links that can be clicked on, detecting and countering chat spam, or being able to disable the visual for said character counter.



After I completed my Unity development tasks, I was also responsible for building a captivating 3D desert landscape map. I was given limited lore/design direction requrements from the stakeholders, and excercised full creative control in developing the extent of the map. I produced extravagant, and twisting mountain passages, a war torn capitol of the past that has begun to be reclaimed by the ocean, and a bustling, lively nomad village, to name just a few elements. Each of the nomad tents can be entered and have unique, hand-crafted interiors. There are also other secrets around the map like hidden mountain passages, and an island with treasure that has a scenic view of the nearby ship graveyard.
